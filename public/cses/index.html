Part 1
Modern Domain, Hosting & Google Workspace

Before anything else can work, the school needs to own its own digital infrastructure. Right now, some emails bounce before they reach students. Files live in someone's personal account instead of belonging to the school. And there's no clean way to give a TA or new hire access to just what they need — it's all or nothing.

This part fixes all of that. It moves CSES onto a modern, reliable foundation it actually controls — email that arrives, a shared drive that belongs to the school, and access that can be managed cleanly. It also makes website hosting free. Everything that comes after gets faster and cheaper once this is in place.

$1,800 – $3,000 About one week · Recommended first step
The problem this solves
Today
  • Email bounces or gets blocked by certain providers — the authentication records that prove "this really is energyschool.com" are missing or misconfigured.
  • Everything runs on one personal account with one login. No way to give someone access to just the folder they need.
  • Aging hosting infrastructure — harder to manage, less secure, costs more than it needs to.
  • Files belong to an individual, not to the institution. If that person leaves, files walk out.
After Part 1
  • Email reliably reaches inboxes. Authentication records set up properly and easy to manage going forward.
  • The school controls its own accounts and domain — add people, remove people, reset access from one place.
  • Website hosting is free, on a faster and more secure platform owned by CSES.
  • Files and infrastructure belong to CSES permanently. No single-person dependency.
Piece A — Modern domain & hosting foundation

Move the school's domain settings to Cloudflare — modern, free, and far more reliable — owned by a CSES account with Andrew as a permissioned admin.

  • DNS and email authentication records (SPF, DKIM, DMARC) properly configured — fixes bouncing at the root
  • Website hosting moved to Cloudflare Pages — free, fast, more secure than current paid hosting
  • Domain registrar account owned by CSES; Andrew maintains through delegated admin access
  • Carefully managed cutover — brief, coordinated, seamless if done right
Piece B — Google Workspace & Drive
  • Google Workspace set up on energyschool.com — the real business version, tied to the domain
  • Account structure: base institutional account plus working account for Administrative Director
  • Shared Drive owned by the organization — files belong to CSES, not any individual
  • Access tiers established: read-only by default, scoped to what each person needs
  • Drop zone for incoming files — teachers hand in recordings without a login or storage burden
  • Storage playbook — folder structure, naming conventions, access map — documented
  • Basic training so the team can operate it confidently
What changes for you
  • Email reliably reaches inboxes instead of bouncing
  • Website hosting becomes free, on a faster and more secure platform
  • The school controls its own accounts and domain — no single-person dependency
  • Access is scoped to what each person needs, read-only by default
  • Files and infrastructure are owned by CSES permanently
  • Every future project gets faster and cheaper because it's building on solid ground
Cost notes

The $1,800–$3,000 covers the whole foundation. Domain/hosting move is roughly $500–$800 of it; Workspace setup, Shared Drive, access rules, skeleton playbook, and training make up the remainder.

CSES pays Google directly for Workspace — Business Starter runs about $7/user/month (~$168/year for two accounts). The domain/hosting platform itself is free. Net effect: the school spends less on infrastructure than it does today, on something far better.

What this does not include (yet)
Migrating the existing pile of files into the new Drive structure. This is deliberately deferred — migrating before the structure and rules are settled means organizing into a system that's still being defined, likely doing it twice. Better to let the new structure prove itself with new material first, then bring the old material across. Rough range when we get there: $2,000 – $4,000, scoped once we can see the actual pile.
Part 2
Ingestion Pipeline

Every time someone signs up for a class, expresses interest in a workshop, or asks about the program, that information arrives scattered — an email here, a form there, a PayPal notification somewhere else. Nancy pulls it together by hand each time, rebuilding the same tally from scratch every time a new workshop runs.

This part puts a single front door on the school. Every inquiry lands in one place, already organized. You can see at a glance who's confirmed, who's paid, who needs a follow-up — without opening twelve tabs. PayPal gets fixed at the same time. And the next workshop? The structure is already there.

$3,000 – $5,000 1–2 weeks · July / August 2026 · Can run alongside Part 1
The problem in plain terms

The email is the form. The inbox is the database. You are the query engine. There's no structure on the way in, so structure has to be imposed by hand on the way out.

How it works today
  • Information arrives through six channels. Some lands in spreadsheets. Some sits in inboxes. Some gets missed.
  • To know the attending count, you open thirty messages and tally by hand.
  • To move the waitlist, you count the NOs separately, figure out how far down to go, and draft individual emails.
  • Every reply arrives as free text in its own thread. Intentions require copying thirty paragraphs into a separate document.
  • Replies trickle in over days — no current count, only a snapshot you rebuild each time you look.
  • Payment status requires opening PayPal and cross-referencing the spreadsheet manually.
  • PayPal itself charges wrong: bad sales tax on deposits, shipping costs on a course.
  • Every workshop rebuilds the tally from scratch. The work is not reusable.
After Part 2
  • One canonical intake form linked from every place inquiries originate.
  • Attending is a yes/no field. Count updates the moment a form is submitted.
  • Waitlist movement is tracked automatically. You know where you stand without a tally.
  • Intentions arrive in their own column — readable by Kate without any copying or pasting.
  • Responses land already sorted: yes-count against capacity, no-count drives the waitlist.
  • Payment status is a field. Updated automatically when PayPal reports a transaction.
  • PayPal configuration audited and fixed as part of this work.
  • The Working with Babies form runs every time you run Working with Babies. Same table. Same counts.
What gets built
  • One canonical intake form linked from every place inquiries originate — website, email signatures, Zoom registration follow-ups
  • Central database (Baserow-based) where every inquiry, registration, and payment lands automatically
  • Status fields per record: new → in conversation → ready to register → registered → paid → in cohort → declined
  • Dashboard view for the daily question: "What needs attention today?"
  • Dashboard view for the weekly question: "Who hasn't followed up on what we sent?"
  • PayPal integration audit: broken sales tax on deposits and wrong shipping costs — fixed alongside the rebuild
  • One-page playbook explaining how it works and how to use it
What changes for you
  • You stop chasing information across systems. The information comes to one place.
  • Email becomes about real questions from real people, not administrative tracking.
  • Nothing falls through the cracks — every inquiry is captured at intake.
  • Hector's PayPal-charges-wrong-thing irritation goes away.
What it does not do (yet)
This is the intake layer. It captures and routes. It does not yet track student progress through coursework or hold the long-term record of every program a student has taken. That's Part 3. The bridge version for collection tasks (Google Forms → Sheets) is free and deployable this week for Working with Babies — but the roster reconciliation problem (is this reply from a deposit-paid student?) only resolves when the SIS gives every student a known record.
Part 3
Student Information System

CSES has been tracking student progress across spreadsheets, attendance sheets, and people's memories for years. It works — until the state auditor asks for module-by-module records, or a student needs a transcript, or someone wants to know which alumni are eligible for advanced training. Each of those questions takes real time to answer manually.

This part builds the authoritative record of who studied what at CSES. Transcripts in minutes. State reports on demand. Liz can answer "what has this student completed?" without asking Nancy to dig through files. It's the foundation that lets the school operate like the serious institution it already is.

  • Frees your and Nancy's time immediately. The administrative weight of tracking, reporting, and answering "what's this student's status?" gets absorbed into the system.

A small, true story about the workshop — and what it costs to run it.

How the week goes

How the week goes

It's late June, and Working with Babies is three weeks out. Kate White flies in on July 8th to teach for five days. Twenty-eight seats. Most of them spoken for — though "most" is doing a lot of work in that sentence, because knowing exactly who's coming would take an afternoon Nancy doesn't have.

Here's the afternoon. The confirmation went out as a group email — a warm note, deposit instructions, the address on Arapahoe — sent to everyone at once. The sending was easy. It's what comes back that isn't. Twenty-eight replies, if she's lucky, trickling in over a week, each one a little different. "Yes! Can't wait — is there parking?" "I'm in, I sent the deposit last week, did it come through?" "Yes, and I'm really hoping we'll cover premature infants, that's why I'm coming." Nancy reads each one and pulls out the yes by hand. But the deposit question she can't answer at all — she has no window into payments, and shouldn't; the financials live with Liz. So "did my deposit come through?" becomes a note to pass along, and the real answer to "who has actually paid?" exists in exactly one place, in one person's view, and nowhere Nancy can see. The two halves of each student — are they coming, have they paid — never sit in the same place.

She keeps a running tally in a document. Twenty-four yeses, maybe twenty-five; one person said yes and then asked a question that sounded like a maybe. Three people haven't replied at all. And which of the yeses have actually paid their deposit, she truly cannot say — that's Liz's to check, when Liz has a moment, against a system only Liz can see. A few seats might be open, which means the waitlist — except there's no real waitlist, not in any ordered sense. No one wrote down when each person signed up, so there's no first-come list to draw from. "Who's next" is whoever Nancy can remember, or scroll back far enough to find. The order gets improvised every time, and each person gets their own note, written by hand.

Then there are the intentions. Kate likes to know what's drawing people — it shapes how she teaches the week. So Nancy sends a second email asking, gathers the replies into a fresh document, and formats them by hand so Kate can read them on the plane. The premature-infants person goes in. So does the NICU nurse, and the one returning to the work after her own birth. It's a lovely document. It will be read once and never opened again.

And the lodging. Boulder is expensive, so nine community members open rooms to visiting students. Their rates change, their availability changes, and the only record is last year's email thread. So Nancy writes to all nine, asking them to confirm — again — what they charge and whether they have space, then relays each answer to the students who asked.

None of it is hard, exactly. It's that every piece lives in Nancy's head and her inbox, and none of it will still be there the next time Working with Babies runs. In a year, she'll do all of it again, from the beginning.

The workshop, for the record, is wonderful. It always is.

How it could go

How the week could go

It's late June, and Working with Babies is three weeks out. The same twenty-eight seats, the same Kate White, the same July 8th.

Weeks ago, one tracked message went out to the whole list at once — not twenty-eight separate emails, but a single send that quietly reports who opened it and who didn't, so a gentle nudge can go only to the people who never saw it. Inside was one link. Not an email to reply to — a form. Are you coming? Yes or no. What's drawing you to the work? Where are you traveling from, and do you need lodging?

The form already knew who some of these people were. Returning students — anyone who'd taken a class before — came in flagged as already in the system, their record recognized rather than rebuilt, no duplicate, no re-keying.

The answers didn't land in an inbox. They landed in one place, already sorted. The yes-count sits at the top, against a capacity of twenty-eight, and it's simply right — not "twenty-four, maybe twenty-five," but right, because every yes is a row, not a sentence someone had to interpret. And every row is stamped with the moment it arrived, so the waitlist orders itself: first to sign up, first in line, no one's memory required. When a person marked no, the next name surfaced on its own — the right next name, by date, not by whoever happened to be remembered.

And the seat itself is gated. A spot isn't held because someone said yes — it's held when the deposit arrives. Payment opens the gate automatically: paid, you're in; unpaid, you're simply not yet, and no one has to notice the gap or chase it, because the gap is closed by default. The exception runs the other way now — letting someone in without payment takes a deliberate, positive approval, instead of an unpaid spot quietly sitting open until someone catches it.

Nancy still can't see the financials, and still shouldn't — that stays with Liz. But she doesn't need to. What shows on the record isn't the money, just the status: a small green word that says paid, set by the system the moment payment lands. The two halves of each student — are they coming, have they paid — finally sit in the same place, without Nancy ever touching the till.

The intentions are already a list. The premature-infants person, the NICU nurse, the one returning after her own birth — all of it in one column, and Kate gets a view of exactly that. No document to assemble, nothing to format. She reads it on the plane. It's still lovely. It just cost no one an evening.

The lodging hosts each keep a record current themselves. When a student asks about a room, the answer is already there.

And here's the part that matters most: next July, when Working with Babies runs again, none of this starts over. The form is still there. The hosts' records are still current. The structure Nancy built once simply runs again — and she spends her June on the things only a person can do.

The workshop, for the record, is wonderful. It always was. That was never the problem.

The teaching was always going to be good. The only question was whether the week before it had to cost so much — whether running a beautiful workshop had to mean rebuilding the same scaffolding by hand, every time, in one person's head.

It doesn't.
